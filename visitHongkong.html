Take a Spiritual Tour of Tokyo's Shrines and Temples


You might associate the Japanese capital with cutting-edge tech, but the city also has a fascinating spiritual heritage. Learn about its unique blend of Zen Buddhism and Shintoism on a tour of the best shrines and temples in Tokyo


28/02/2023



When visiting a country that's so famous for its secular exports such as sushi, manga and electronics, you might be surprised by how much religion is on display, even in a hyper-modern metropolis like Tokyo. The city is filled with dozens, if not hundreds, of temples and shrines, practically on every street corner.

These ornate buildings give insight into Japan's special blend of spirituality, which has been shaped by two different but complementary religious traditions. The first is Japan's homegrown strand of animism, called Shintoism. Its temples, known as jinja (shrines), can be identified by torii gates - simple archways that denote the separation of the mundane from the sacred, often painted in a vibrant shiny red.





The other main religion is Buddhism: various sects, including Zen, were imported from India and China in the 6th century. Buddhist temples also have gates (sanmon), but these tend to be far more impressive, multi-storey affairs, accompanied by ornate towers or pagodas.

These two religions are practised both separately and together in a mixture of traditions known as shinbutsu-shūgō. No visit to Japan is complete without exploring how these two religions have shaped the country's culture and history - from Samurai warriors to Japanese garden design. Take a trip around some of Tokyo's must-see temples and shrines to immerse yourself in Japanese spirituality.


Central Tokyo: temple meditation and forested shrines

You won't struggle to find the Zōjō-ji, right beneath the iconic Tokyo Tower in Shiba Park. Dating back to the 1390s as the main temple of Jōdo Buddhism, it was rebuilt after World War II bombings.

Look out for the poignant Garden for Unborn Children, where offerings are dedicated to the growth and health of children, and are left to Jizō Bosatsu, the Buddhist protector of women and children.





If you'd like to take part in some Zen meditation yourself, head to the Kōrin-in sub-temple, a couple of train stops from Shibuya, where several zazen meditation sessions are offered every weekday at 7am. Beginners are welcome to join, and explanations are in Japanese and English.




Atago Jinja was built on top of a hill to protect its wooden structure from fire, and a visit is not for the faint hearted



Also in the central areas you'll find the rustic Shinto shrine, Atago Jinja. It was built on top of a hill to protect its wooden structure from fire, and a visit is not for the faint hearted. You must climb up a steep flight of 86 stairs - dubbed the 'stone steps to success' - to access its delights. Visit the central-west area of Shibuya for the city's most impressive Shinto shrine: Meiji Jingu. The word 'Jingu' indicates an Imperial connection, and this shrine is dedicated to Emperor Meiji, Japan's first constitutional monarch, who died in 1912, and his wife, Empress Shoken. It's a popular place for traditional Japanese-style weddings, as you will no doubt see on your visit, and it's also known as an urban woodland oasis, surrounded by some 36,000 trees. Don't miss Senso-ji, the city's oldest Buddhist temple, built when its home in Asakusa was a separate town, rather than part of the urban sprawl. Today it's known for its riverside locations and local crafts - as well as for its temple, which draws 30 million worshippers per year.






Suburban highlights: giant mythological reptiles
For a truly unusual take on religion, take the train south to Tamagawa Station for the Tamagawa Sengen Shrine, where visitors worship Godzilla. Although the shrine dates back to the 1180s and has some unique architecture in the Sengen-zukuri style, its main draw today is as a filming location for the 2016 movie, Shin Godzilla, and visitors can buy religious objects bearing the lizard's image.





Finally, and particularly if you find yourself in Tokyo this May, you must go to the Kanda Myojin Shrine - home to Shinto worship for 1,300 years. The Kanda Matsuri (or Kanda Festival) held here biannually is one of the three most important Shinto festivals in Japan - involving 200 mikoshi (portable shrines), as well as floats, musicians and dancers. Even visiting just half of these temples will give you a fantastic overview of the spiritual culture of Japan. After a hard few days of sightseeing, put your feet up at your hotel - we recommend staying in the Shibuya, Asakusa and Shiba areas, which are close to several of the central temples. Breathe out and relax.
